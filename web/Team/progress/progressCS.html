Week 7:

Date: 10/06/17
Total hours: 10
Summary of Efforts:
This week was used to compile the schematic for the motherboard. In this process, decisions on what was going to be connected to which pins of the microcontroller and how. The interfaces that gave us the most trouble, and will continue to, include the wifi module, the USB Type-C power circuitry, the NFC circuitry, and the programming interface. Most of the issues are in absences of knowledge in what pins to connect, even when the communication interface was chosen long beforehand. For example: the NFC chip we chose had a serial port; however, it also had many other wires that were not mentioned included a 7 bit bi-directional data bus line, 2 bit address line, and host of other pins. Many of these signal pins were incorporated into an antenna circuit we had overlooked. Since we needed more information to approach that circuit (values of passives, part information, etc), and could not find a vendor to purchase its development board, we had to completely replace this element of our design with the next best option. This new part, is the PN532, as decided in our parts analysis for early in the project. The end result is that we will incorporate its dev boards into our project, since the antenna circuitry and closely matched impedence elements are a non-trivial design task.

Earlier in the week, to fill in time Rebecca and I had been spending on schematic design, Vignesh and Ziad had approached the assembly of the input card. Though this element is not immediately to our nearest deadlines, it is still excellent to have it assembled. As neither Vignesh nor Ziad had training soldering surface mount packages, I had helped coach them through the process and give them feedback on how to improve their work and make the job easier.

Another component of this weeks major works includes having designed a breakout board for USB Type-C. I considered this a great feat and had though I had done very well, but thankfully before I had ordered it had recognized that the USB Type-C socket I had designed the board for was not commonly marketed - meaning my board will need to be redesigned. This is a frustrating set-back, but there is no choice but to make the board correctly, or not at all. The part that is commonly marketed is very similar, but has through hole pins for the top side of the USB cable rather than pads for both sides. What had redeemed the frustrations of the day was the compilation of a Gantt chart. I had never made a Gantt chart before, but I am learning that in doing so we can see the tasks ahead of us very easily and can make much more educated decisions about the priorities of our efforts.

Week 9:

Date: 10/20/17
Total Hours: 25+
In this week we finalized the production of our pcb and all of its glory. Many traces were rerouted across the board in association with the input card socket and nfc and overall, many fewer traces cross than before. The silkscreen layer was augmented with labels for all of the debug signals and a few addition breakout vias for checking the power levels and probing signals. The one spare pin on our micro controller was routed to a debug LED so that we could get simple binary feedback while we encorporate our LCD screen. The crystal circuit and ground layer accommodations were made so that we will hopefully be able to have a reliable 16MHz external clock source, rather than the simple RC resonater.
Additional to Rebecca and I's cooperatie efforts on the PCB I had also attempted to get a functional prototype for the weight sensing circuit. I was able to get the development environment set up on my computer, the microcontroller programming and sending out SPI signals, and data being read back from the HX711 load amplifier; however, the values did not seem to change when the bar was stressed. I am uncertain if this is due to inpropper operation or faulty equiptment, but I suspect the latter. The HX711 requires 25 or 27 bits to be sent in order to send back the data and set the gain value, but the HAL software from STM only supports bytewise communication. To help mitigate the issue I had written drivers for bit banging the comminication until I can handle the nuiance of switching the port pins' operations on-the-fly. These drivers will be tested this weekend.

Week 10:

Date: 10/27/17
Total Hours: 8
In spite of all odds, our motherboard PCB came back from China this week, Thursday evening. Though the expectation is that it would take at least 7 buisness days, production and shipping happened in a miraculous in a record 5 calendar days. The most exiciting of our news thus far, Thursday night was spent soldering on the power elements of the circuit board and examining the board for defects. There were a few minor issues with the board and one major issue - an embarrasing footprint error that could have been avoided if we had printed 1:1 as advized before. I am not surprised that there are issues, but I am irked that this one got through - the wifi card pin spacing is off by 100 mils. Though the problem is not a showstopper by any means it is a possible grounds for a new board if other improvements merit a respin. Errors asside, the results are beautiful. The board spacing looks great and the silk screen and traces are all continuous and unflawed. After logging the issues I had seen I had hooked up the circuit for load testing at 15 Ohms on the 3.3 volt line. There it would stay for nearly 24 hours.
Other exciting breakthroughs did occur this week, especially in terms of software. By wiring up the LCD screen as a 5 volt circuit we were able to get reliable communication for debug. With this result I was able to write a simple function to get better real-time feedback on the weight sensor feedback I was trying to read. Upon closer investigation it appears as though the connections to the weight sensor are floating - a typically challenge of trying to wire MTA-100 connectors. I had tried rewiring the connections, and will resume testing of the weight sensor next week. On the same weight sensor topic, I also submitted supporting parts for the weight sesnors down to Chuck in the machine shop. The parts include small linking components that space out the load cell from its mounting surface, the platforms that will support the weight, and also our flat inner layer, the surface. The platforms, not present in our design originally, are round and help focus pressure down onto the load cell to give more accurate weight readings.
